# Persona & Voice Guide

This document defines the personal brand, voice, and copywriting rules for ddlawson.com. All site content — homepage, work history, blog posts, project descriptions, meta text — should read like one person wrote it.

## Identity

David D Lawson is a practitioner who leads, not a leader who used to practice.

The career arc — co-founding a travel startup, running security programmes at enterprise scale, leading customer success teams, now managing Technical Account Management across EMEA at Wiz — is the differentiator. Most people in TAM leadership don't have hands-on startup founding, security engineering, and CS operations in their background. That breadth is the positioning.

**One-line positioning:** I close the gap between what engineering builds and what customers actually need.

This is not a "bridge" metaphor. It's a statement of function. The value is in the closing — understanding both sides deeply enough to make the connection work.

## Voice Principles

### 1. Lead with the point

Open with the claim, the observation, or the tension. Never open with throat-clearing ("In today's landscape...", "As we all know...", "It's no secret that..."). If the first sentence could be deleted without losing meaning, delete it.

**Do:** "The QBR is a relic."
**Don't:** "One thing I've noticed over the years is that QBRs might not be as effective as we once thought."

### 2. Speak from experience, not theory

Ground claims in specific situations you've been in. Name the company, the team size, the metric, the thing that went wrong. Generalisations weaken the voice. If you can't back a claim with a specific memory, reconsider whether to make it.

**Do:** "At Veracode, I worked with customers who had wildly different adoption rates for the same product."
**Don't:** "Many organisations struggle with adoption."

### 3. Take a position

State what you believe and why. Hedge only when the uncertainty is genuine, not as a social reflex. "In my experience" is fine when introducing a non-obvious claim. "I think maybe perhaps" is not.

**Do:** "Scaling CS requires fundamentally changing the operating model, not just adding headcount."
**Don't:** "It could be argued that CS teams might benefit from rethinking their approach."

### 4. Write for the reluctant reader

Assume the reader is busy and sceptical. Every paragraph must earn the next one. Use short paragraphs, concrete examples, and clear structure (headers, numbered lists, bold key phrases). If a section feels like filler, cut it.

### 5. Stay human

Use plain language. Occasional profanity is fine when it lands ("gives a damn"). Don't sanitise the voice into corporate-safe blandness. But don't force edge either — the tone is confident and direct, not aggressive.

## Vocabulary

### Use freely

- Specific verbs: led, built, shipped, cut, reduced, identified, replaced, launched
- Concrete nouns: team, pipeline, dashboard, deployment, remediation, onboarding
- Outcome language: "reduced churn by", "cut response time from X to Y", "grew adoption to"
- First person: "I", "we", "my team"

### Use sparingly

- "Strategic" — only when describing actual strategy work, not as a generic intensifier
- "Leverage" — prefer "use"
- "Ecosystem" — prefer "stack" or "toolchain" unless referring to a partner ecosystem
- "Journey" — prefer "experience" or "process"

### Never use

- "Orchestrated" — vague and overused
- "Spearheaded" — CV filler
- "Cross-functional collaboration" — describe what actually happened instead
- "Drive/drove [noun]" as a substitute for specifics ("drove adoption" → "grew adoption from 40% to 72%")
- "Passionate about" — show the passion through the work
- "Thought leader" / "thought leadership"
- "Seamless" — nothing is seamless; describe what makes it smooth
- "Comprehensive" — describe the scope instead
- "High-impact" — describe the impact instead
- "Value realization" — say what the customer actually gained
- "Stakeholder alignment" — say who you aligned and on what
- "Best-in-class" / "world-class" / "cutting-edge"
- "Empower" / "enable" (as buzzwords) — say what you made possible

## Audience

The site serves three audiences, in priority order:

1. **Peers and practitioners** — CS leaders, TAMs, security programme managers who want to compare notes and learn from someone doing similar work. They read the blog and judge credibility by specificity.
2. **Hiring managers and recruiters** — scanning the work page for trajectory, scope, and impact. They need to see progression and quantified results, not job-description language.
3. **Potential collaborators** — founders, product leaders, and engineers who might want to work with someone who understands the customer side. They read the projects section and blog to gauge thinking quality.

All three audiences are allergic to corporate-speak. Write for the first group and the other two will follow.

## Content-Specific Rules

### Work history summaries

Write in the pattern: **did [specific thing] → [measurable result]**. One sentence on what you did, one on what changed because of it. Strip any word that could apply to anyone in any role.

### Blog posts

- Titles should make a claim or create tension, not describe a topic ("The Customer Success Playbook Doesn't Scale" > "Thoughts on Scaling Customer Success").
- Descriptions should promise a specific insight, not gesture at a topic area.
- Vary closing CTAs — not every post needs "reach out." Some can end on the insight. Some can ask a question. Some can point to a related project.
- Watch for repeated phrases across posts ("the honest truth/answer/reality", "here's what/why"). Once is a device; three times is a tic.

### Project descriptions

Name what the project does, not what category it falls into. "AI-powered tools" tells the reader nothing. What does it actually help someone do?

### Meta titles and descriptions

- Meta titles should hook, not label. "David D Lawson | B2B SaaS Customer Experience Professional" is a LinkedIn headline. Lead with what makes you different.
- Meta descriptions should make the reader want to click, not summarise the page.

## Language & Spelling

Use British English throughout: organisation, programme, optimise, behaviour, colour, specialise. This applies to all site copy, blog posts, and meta text.
