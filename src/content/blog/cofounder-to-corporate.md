In 2017, I co-founded a travel startup in Turin. Two years later, I was a Security Programme Manager at an enterprise cybersecurity company. The whiplash was real.

## What startup life taught me

Running a startup forces a kind of radical pragmatism. When you're the technical lead, the marketing department, and the customer support team simultaneously, you develop an instinct for prioritisation that no MBA can replicate.

The most valuable lesson: **done is a feature.** A shipped product that's 80% right generates more learning in a week than a perfect product generates in a quarter of planning. I carried this instinct into every corporate role that followed, and it served me well every time.

## What the enterprise taught me

Enterprise moves slower, but it moves heavier. Decisions compound in ways they don't at a startup. A poorly scoped project doesn't waste a sprint — it wastes a quarter and burns trust across three departments.

The adaptation wasn't learning to move slower. It was learning to build alignment *before* moving. In a startup, you can course-correct in an afternoon. In enterprise, the cost of correction scales with the number of people who need to change direction.

## The leadership lesson

The biggest surprise was that the leadership skills transfer directly — but the *application* is entirely different.

In a startup, leadership is about energy and conviction. You're asking people to believe in something that doesn't exist yet. In enterprise, leadership is about translation and persistence. You're helping people see how a new approach connects to outcomes they already care about, and you're repeating that connection until it becomes common knowledge.

Both require the same foundation: genuine care for the people you're leading and relentless clarity about where you're going. The difference is the surface — and the surface matters more than most people admit.

## What I'd tell my younger self

Don't try to replicate the startup experience inside a corporate environment. The constraints are different. The opportunities are different. Instead, identify the *principles* that worked — speed, customer proximity, ruthless prioritisation — and find the corporate expression of each.

A two-week experiment in a startup becomes a 48-hour proof of concept in enterprise. A founder-to-customer conversation becomes a weekly voice-of-customer session with the product team. The form changes. The substance doesn't.

---

*The form changes. The substance doesn't. That's the thing nobody tells you during the transition — and the thing that makes it survivable.*
