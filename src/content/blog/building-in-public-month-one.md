On January 1st, I started sharing my work publicly. Not polished case studies — the actual work. Half-formed ideas, abandoned drafts, failed experiments. It was uncomfortable.

## The premise

The hypothesis was simple: sharing process, not just outcomes, would attract better conversations than a curated portfolio ever could. I'd seen founders and designers do this successfully. I wanted to see if it applied to customer experience work — a field that usually keeps its methods behind closed doors.

## What happened

**Week one** was mostly overcoming the instinct to qualify everything. Every post felt like it needed a disclaimer: *this is a work in progress, I know it's rough, don't judge the final product by this.* I posted anyway.

**Week two**, something shifted. A connection messaged me about a customer segmentation framework I'd shared in an early draft. They'd adapted it for their own team within 48 hours. That single interaction generated more valuable feedback than months of internal strategy documents.

**Week three**, I started getting the question I wasn't expecting: "Why would you share this?" The answer I landed on is that the internet is full of polished success stories. The gap — the thing people actually need — is the messy middle. The iterations. The dead ends. The moment you realise approach A is wrong and need to pivot to approach B.

**Week four**, I settled into a rhythm. Three posts a week. Mix of frameworks, raw notes, and short reflections. Engagement was modest but *deep* — the people who responded were people I actually wanted to talk to.

## What I'm carrying forward

The biggest surprise was how much *I* benefited from the act of publishing. Writing forces clarity. Publishing forces honesty. The combination is a better thinking tool than any framework I've used.

Month two starts now. I'm planning to open up the operational playbooks I've built over the years — customer health scoring, escalation frameworks, cross-functional meeting cadences. The stuff that actually moves the needle but never makes it into conference talks.

*If you've been considering building in public, this is your sign. The discomfort fades fast. The upside compounds.*
